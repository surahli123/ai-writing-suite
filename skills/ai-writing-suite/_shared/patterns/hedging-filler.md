# Hedging & Filler Tells

The model adds words that add no meaning, and qualifies claims until they assert
nothing. These slow the writing down and give it a tutorial-script, committee-
written feel.

---

### H1 — Filler phrases

- **Tell:** Mechanical padding that adds words without meaning: "It is important
  to note that," "In order to," "Due to the fact that," "At this point in time,"
  "In the event that," "The system has the ability to," "In terms of," "The
  reality is that."
- **Fix:** Cut to the point. "It is important to note that the data shows" → "The
  data shows." "In order to" → "To." "Due to the fact that" → "Because."
- **Sources:** aboudjem (§23), avoid-ai (filler phrases), anti-vibe, stop-slop.

---

### H2 — Excessive / stacked hedging

- **Tell:** Over-qualifying: "could potentially possibly," "it might perhaps be
  argued." And hedge-stacked predictions where a modal piles on a hedge adverb:
  "could potentially create," "may eventually unlock," "might ultimately
  transform." Each hedge cancels the next, leaving a cautious-sounding sentence
  that asserts nothing.
- **Fix:** Pick one. "could create" or "potentially creates," not both. Keep
  nuance where it matters; remove it where it only weakens the sentence.
- **Sources:** aboudjem (§24), avoid-ai (hedge-stacked predictions), anti-vibe
  (over-balanced tone), stop-slop.

---

### H3 — Generic positive conclusions

- **Tell:** Vague upbeat endings: "The future looks bright," "Exciting times lie
  ahead," "continues its journey toward excellence," "a step in the right
  direction," "As we move forward," "Only time will tell."
- **Fix:** Cut them. If the piece needs a closing thought, make it specific to the
  argument. "The company plans to open two more locations next year."
- **Sources:** blader (P24), aboudjem (§25), avoid-ai (generic conclusions),
  anti-vibe.

---

### H4 — Generic future-narrative closers

- **Tell:** A closer that's grammatically a prediction but contains no testable
  content: modal (may / could / will / is poised to) + "become" + "one of the
  most [adjective]" + (narrative / story / trend / chapter / movement). "May
  become one of the most important narratives of the next market cycle."
- **Fix:** Pick the falsifiable version. "DePIN compute may exceed AWS spot
  pricing for parallel workloads by 2027" is a prediction; the original is not.
- **Sources:** avoid-ai (future-narrative closers).

---

### H5 — Confidence-calibration phrases

- **Tell:** Telling the reader how to feel about a fact instead of letting it
  speak: "It's worth noting that," "Interestingly," "Surprisingly," "Notably,"
  "Importantly," "Certainly," "Undoubtedly." Plus persuasive-authority tropes
  that announce depth: "the real question is," "at its core," "fundamentally,"
  "make no mistake," "the truth is."
- **Fix:** Cut the cue and lead with the substance. One "notably" in 2,000 words
  is fine; three in 500 is emphasis-stacking — flag by density.
- **Sources:** avoid-ai (confidence calibration), blader (P27 persuasive
  authority), anti-vibe.

---

### H6 — Self-labeling significance

- **Tell:** After a list, pointing back at one item to label it
  clever/contrarian/surprising/key: "That last move is the contrarian one," "This
  is the interesting part," "Here's where it gets clever." The label does the work
  the content should have done.
- **Fix:** Cut the labeling sentence and let the explanation do the work, or
  restructure so the highlighted item carries weight on its own.
- **Sources:** avoid-ai (self-labeling significance).

---

### H7 — Signposting / announcements

- **Tell:** Announcing what the text is about to do instead of doing it: "Let's
  dive in," "Let's explore," "Here's what you need to know," "Now let's look at,"
  "Without further ado," "But first."
- **Fix:** Say the thing. The reader doesn't need a tour guide.
- **Before:** Let's dive into how caching works in Next.js. Here's what you need
  to know.
- **After:** Next.js caches data at several layers: request memoization, the data
  cache, and the router cache.
- **Sources:** aboudjem (§28), avoid-ai (chatbot artifacts / "let's"
  constructions), anti-vibe (signposting), stop-slop (meta-joiners).

---

### H8 — "Let's" false-collaborative openers

- **Tell:** "Let's + verb" used as a transition rather than a genuine invitation:
  "Let's take a look," "Let's break this down," "Let's examine." A false-
  collaborative ease-in that delays the point.
- **Fix:** Start with the point. (Related to H7 but broader than "let's dive in.")
- **Sources:** avoid-ai ("let's" constructions).

---

### H9 — Vague endorsement ("worth [verb]ing")

- **Tell:** A generic thumbs-up substituting for a specific reason: "worth
  reading," "worth paying attention to," "worth a look," "worth your time."
- **Fix:** Say *why* it matters instead.
- **Sources:** avoid-ai (vague endorsement).

---

### H10 — Vague abstraction

- **Tell:** Abstract wording where a concrete fact belongs: "improve
  collaboration across stakeholders," "enhance strategic alignment," "deliver
  meaningful outcomes."
- **Fix:** Replace with what actually happened, exists, or should be done. "Reduce
  setup time from thirty minutes to five." "Make the tradeoff explicit before
  implementation starts."
- **Sources:** anti-vibe (vague abstraction), avoid-ai, stop-slop (be specific).
