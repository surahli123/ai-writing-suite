# Structure — lead with the point, then support it

> **Generic best-practice entry.** Distilled from the four MIT reference repos
> (see `Sources`). Open-source KB; company forks swap via the same slot
> (`README.md`).

## Principle

Structure is the order in which ideas arrive. Most professional writing should be
**top-down**: state the conclusion first, then the reasons, then the detail. A
reader who stops after the first paragraph should still have the answer.

## Moves

- **BLUF — bottom line up front.** Open with the decision, result, or ask. Don't
  make the reader earn the point by reading to the end.
- **One idea per paragraph.** A paragraph that changes topic mid-way should be
  two paragraphs.
- **Use the natural number of points.** Don't force everything into a "rule of
  three." Sometimes the answer is one reason; sometimes four. Forced triads read
  as machine-generated.
- **Headings describe content, not ceremony.** "Migration steps" beats
  "Challenges and Legacy" or "The Road Ahead" — stock headings signal filler.
- **Match shape to channel.** A tweet is one hook, no headers. A memo is BLUF +
  sections. A README is task-ordered. Don't pour memo structure into a tweet.

## Before / After

- **Before:** In this document we will explore several considerations. First, we
  provide background. [...three paragraphs...] Therefore, we recommend Option B.
- **After:** Recommendation: ship Option B. It is 2x cheaper and reuses the
  existing pipeline. Background and the two rejected options follow.

## When this matters most

Decision memos, proposals, exec updates. The reader's time budget is short and
front-loaded — spend the first sentence on the answer.
