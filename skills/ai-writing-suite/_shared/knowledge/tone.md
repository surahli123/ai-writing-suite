# Tone — sound like a person, calibrated to the context

> **Generic best-practice entry.** Distilled from the four MIT reference repos
> (see `Sources`). Open-source KB; company forks swap via the same slot
> (`README.md`).

## Principle

Tone is the voice the reader hears. Good professional tone is direct, warm
enough to be human, and free of both corporate stiffness and AI's over-eager
cheerfulness. The target is "a competent colleague explaining something," not a
press release and not a chatbot.

## Moves

- **Drop the sycophancy.** "Great question!", "I'd be happy to help", "Certainly!"
  are chatbot tics. Start with the substance.
- **Vary sentence length.** Uniformly medium sentences read as machine-generated
  (low burstiness). Mix a short punch with a longer explanation.
- **No hype inflation.** "Revolutionary," "game-changing," "seamless,"
  "robust" — claims the text can't back up. State what it actually does.
- **Hedge only where there's real uncertainty.** Stacked hedges ("it might
  perhaps be somewhat possible") signal an AI covering itself. Say what you know
  plainly; flag the one thing you don't.
- **Match formality to the channel.** A Slack update is looser than a board memo.
  Don't write Slack like a contract or a memo like Slack.

## Before / After

- **Before:** Great question! This is a really powerful, game-changing approach
  that could potentially help streamline your workflow seamlessly.
- **After:** This cuts the manual step. You paste the link; it returns the draft.

## When this matters most

Anything customer- or leadership-facing, and anything passed off as your own
writing. Tone is what makes a reader trust — or distrust — the content.
